// 01 — The Problem

Every Change Initiative Started From Zero

The MBTA's Quality, Compliance and Oversight Change Management Office was operating during a period of significant transformation — regulatory-driven policy changes, leadership shifts, and new strategic directions, all requiring coordinated, effective communication with a large and diverse frontline workforce.

The core problem was structural: there was no centralized source of truth. Who the workforce was, what they did, how to reach them, what they were subject to — all of that intelligence existed in fragments. HRCMIS. NeoGov. Tableau. Departmental spreadsheets. Personal drives. Every time a new change initiative kicked off, the team had to rebuild the whole picture from scratch, duplicating weeks of analysis that someone had already done for the last project.

Without a unified view, it was nearly impossible to assess the true scope of impact of any change, identify the right communication channels for different employee groups, or plan targeted interventions for the roles that needed them most. Change initiatives were at risk of failing not because the strategy was wrong, but because the team didn't have the visibility to execute.

"All of this information existed somewhere across the agency — it just never lived together. That gap was costing us time, clarity, and impact on every single project."

// 02 — My Role & the Idea

I Saw the Pattern. I Built the Solution.

I originated the concept for the Workforce Segmentation Database. Having watched teams repeatedly scramble to reconstruct the same workforce intelligence before each change project, I proposed building a reusable, maintainable source of truth that would eliminate that friction permanently.

I then led a small data team to bring the concept to life — designing the data model, coordinating data collection across departments, managing iterative development and validation cycles, and building stakeholder alignment from the C-suite down to department managers. The work demanded equal parts technical execution and organizational diplomacy.

The goal: a 360° picture of the MBTA's frontline workforce that captured not just who people were on paper, but how they worked, what they were entitled to, what they were subject to, and — critically — how best to reach and communicate with them during change.

// 03 — The Build

Four Phases. One Source of Truth.

The build followed a structured process: data consolidation, standardization, visualization, and stakeholder alignment. We extracted workforce data from HRCMIS, NeoGov, budget headcount files, and departmental sources — cleaning, transforming, and normalizing into a relational structure that could support real analysis at scale.

The heart of the work was role type standardization. We developed a job code-to-role-type mapping taxonomy that collapsed hundreds of varied job titles into a consistent organizational taxonomy — Field Operations, Supervisor/Middle Management, Compliance/Inspection, and more — enabling meaningful cross-department comparison for the first time. Our first iteration taught us that job code descriptions alone were too broad; we refined the logic to incorporate position-level data and department context, producing far more accurate results.

The final product was an interactive Tableau dashboard that gave change managers real-time visibility into workforce composition, safety status, union affiliation, training profiles, and more — all filterable by role, department, or business unit. Exactly the tool the team needed, built to last.

// 04 — Navigating Resistance

The Hardest Part Was Never Technical.

Multiple departments were skeptical about sharing their data. There was real territorialism — teams had built and maintained their own information and weren't sure this effort was anything more than an attempt to audit or replicate what they already owned.

I led a series of conversations across the organization — C-suite to front-line managers — with a single consistent message: this tool is not replacing anything you have. It's connecting existing information to serve the workforce better. We positioned the database as a shared resource that would save every department time on their own change efforts, not a competing system.

// 05 — Impact

Research That Used to Take Days Now Takes Minutes.

The database has supported more than five major change initiatives at the MBTA, dramatically cutting the workforce research cycle and enabling more precisely targeted communications with frontline employees. Where teams once spent days rebuilding workforce intelligence from scratch, the tool delivers it in minutes.

PPE / Boot Entitlement

Instantly identified all affected roles by union affiliation, job type, and essential status — cutting research from days to hours.

Hours of Service (DPU)

Surfaced every safety-sensitive position across departments for targeted compliance communications and impact assessment.

Digital Compliance

Mapped roles by device access and Wi-Fi restrictions — enabling offline-first strategies for employees without digital access.

Beyond individual projects, the tool has fundamentally changed how the QCO team approaches change planning. It enables a rapid assessment of the lift associated with any given initiative — who is affected, how many, through what channels, with what constraints — before a single communication is drafted.

// 06 — Lessons Learned

Data Work Is People Work.

This project reinforced something I've come to believe deeply: the hardest part of any data initiative is rarely the data. It's the trust, the framing, and the relationships. Organizational diplomacy was as central to this project's success as any technical decision we made.

On the technical side, our first iteration taught us that job code descriptions alone are too coarse a signal for meaningful role classification. Moving to position-level mapping produced substantially more accurate results — but required more validation cycles with stakeholders. That tradeoff between speed and precision is something I'd plan for explicitly from the start next time.

Starting with four pilot departments rather than attempting agency-wide coverage was the right call. It let us refine the model, demonstrate value, and build credibility before scaling — making the case for expansion far easier to make. The database is still growing, still improving, and still delivering value on every initiative it touches.
