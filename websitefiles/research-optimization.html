// 01 — The Challenge

A World-Class Research Library Nobody Could Navigate

J-PAL exists to change the world through evidence — randomized evaluations and rigorous research that shapes how governments and organizations fight poverty across the globe. The work is genuinely important. But important work doesn't help anyone if the people who need it can't find it.

The legacy povertyactionlab.org site was built in 2016 for a fraction of the content it was now trying to house. Hundreds of research resources, toolkits, and publications had accumulated over the years, with no taxonomy designed to manage that scale. Search was weak. Discovery was frustrating. And the tools to fix it were locked away from the people who most needed to use them.

A key partner donation created the opportunity — and the constraint. Three months to redesign the research experience, rebuild the backend, and launch. I got to work immediately.

"Resources have value only if they can be found. That one principle was the north star for every decision I made on this project."

// 02 — The Strategy

Think About Who's Searching, Then Build Backwards

Before touching a single design file, I spent time understanding the users: who was coming to J-PAL's research resources, what they were trying to accomplish, and where the current experience was losing them. Researchers, policy practitioners, donors, journalists — each with different needs and different mental models for how to navigate a research archive.

From there, I mapped a new information architecture that accounted for the current catalogue and room to scale. Improved taxonomy, better search, a landing page redesign that helped users orient themselves immediately. Then I went into multiple rounds of stakeholder feedback with the internal team before we moved into development.

The resulting page was more dynamic, simpler to navigate, and built to grow — not just for the content J-PAL had, but for everything they'd add over the next five to ten years.

// 03 — The Infrastructure Fix

The Backend Was Holding Everyone Back

The research resource redesign was just the start. While deep in the project, I identified a bigger problem: the toolkit and resource templates across the site were completely inflexible. They could only be edited by the central web team, meaning regional offices around the world had to submit a web ticket every time they needed to make a change to their own content. That's not a content strategy — that's a bottleneck.

I led a competitive analysis of how other organizations handled this problem, ran team discussions to map the full scope of the need, and advocated for a backend solution that would actually fix it. What we implemented was J-PAL's rollout of Drupal's Layout Builder — giving teams around the world the ability to make templated changes to landing pages without touching HTML or CSS, and without waiting on a web ticket queue.

The result: seven regional offices could now update, publish, and customize their own content independently. That's a workflow transformation, not just a UX improvement.

// 04 — The Bigger Picture

20 Years of Impact, Finally Visible

As part of J-PAL's 20th anniversary, regional offices wanted to tell their stories — the donors they'd engaged, the policies they'd influenced, the research that had made it into the real world. That required a new kind of content template: something visually compelling, flexible enough to work across seven very different regional contexts, and fast enough to build under anniversary pressure.

I designed the first of these anniversary templates — the "Southeast Asia at 10" microsite — and built it as a reusable format that other offices could adapt. Rich visuals, structured narrative flow, optimized for donor engagement. A template that honored the weight of the work it was showcasing.

The through-line across all of it: get out of the way of the content. J-PAL's research is powerful on its own. My job was to build a digital environment where it could actually land.
