// 01 — The Challenge

When the Store Is the Campaign

Campaign merchandise isn't just merch — it's a fundraising channel, a community signal, and an engagement tool all at once. When a supporter buys a shirt, they're not just getting a shirt. They're buying into the campaign, telling their friends, and putting money directly into the fight.

The Warren for President store had a loyal audience and a strong product. What it needed was an experience worthy of both. The site wasn't converting the way it should, the navigation wasn't surfacing the right products to the right people, and there was a significant opportunity sitting untouched in the checkout flow.

I was brought in to fix all of it — and to do it fast, because in a presidential campaign, there's no such thing as "let's revisit next quarter."

"A campaign store is basically a startup e-commerce operation running at full speed inside one of the most intense work environments on the planet. I loved every second of it."

// 02 — The Overhaul

Redesign First, Then Build on Top

I led a full site redesign starting in late September 2019 — new visual identity, new category architecture, new product layouts, new copy. Working with UX/UI designers and the development team, we rebuilt the store's look and feel from the ground up, using conversion data and analytics to guide every structural decision.

The category and subcategory layouts were especially data-driven: where products sat on the page, how promotions were surfaced, what the first thing a visitor saw when they landed — all of it tested and iterated. The result was a site that didn't just look better. It performed better at every step of the funnel.

We also built out a "You May Also Like" cross-merchandising strategy across all product pages — giving visitors relevant suggestions based on what they were already looking at, and pulling them deeper into the catalogue rather than letting them bounce after one item.

// 03 — The Big Idea

The Checkout Module That Changed Everything

This is the one I'm most proud of. During the checkout process, we introduced a two-part contribution module that gave supporters the option to make an additional donation to the campaign before completing their purchase. Clean, non-intrusive, frictionless to opt into — and enormously effective.

The module generated over $500,000 in campaign contributions — without adding any overhead, any new products, or any additional traffic. It was pure conversion optimization: finding value that was already there and building a path to capture it.

It's the kind of result that sounds simple in retrospect and is anything but simple to execute. Getting the UX right so it feels like a natural part of the flow, not a desperate ask — that's the work.

// 04 — What I Learned

Good UX Is Just Respect for the User's Time

What this project reinforced for me is that conversion optimization isn't about tricks — it's about removing friction and building trust. Every element of the redesign was in service of one thing: making it easier for someone who already wanted to engage to actually do it.

The checkout module worked because it didn't feel like an ambush. It felt like an opportunity that made sense in context. That's the difference between dark patterns and good design — and it's a line I think about on every project I work on.
